Spend to Impress Yourself: Personal Joy Over Universal Formulas

The formula that doesn't exist

One person's "waste of money" is another person's reason to work. The $200 dinner, the $3,000 bike, the aggressive savings rate that friends call joyless — any of these can be exactly right, for the right person, and exactly wrong for their neighbor. The universal formula for good spending is that there isn't one — and this chapter is about what replaces it: a two-blade discipline that spends more than most budgets on what qualifies, and less on everything that doesn't.

The smuggled values in every budget

Most budgeting advice smuggles in someone else's values, dressed as arithmetic: lattes are frivolous (says who?), travel is enriching (always?), cars are waste, homes are virtue. Follow any famous formula closely and you can hear a specific person's preferences being universalized — usually the author's.

Housel's The Art of Spending Money1 pushes back at the root: the point of money is a life you find worth living, and contentment comes from figuring out what genuinely delivers that for you — whether it's luxury dining, an ultralight bike, or the deep satisfaction of a fat savings account. That last one deserves its explicit inclusion, because frugality-shaming is just the smuggling running in reverse: for some people, saving is the pleasure — security as felt joy, the growing NEST as the thing itself — and that's allowed too. The test is never the category. It's whether the spending, honestly audited (Spending as a Mirror's mirror), actually delivers to the person doing it.

The two blades

The practical discipline has two blades, and both matter — most budgets fail by using neither.

Spend lavishly on what you truly love. Identified honestly — via the mirror of Spending as a Mirror, not via what photographs well — the things that genuinely deliver deserve real money, spent without apology or guilt-laundering. This isn't a budget's indulgence clause; it's load-bearing structure: a budget with real pleasure in it survives (the same adherence logic as Snowball, Volume 1 Avalanche vs. Snowball vs. Intelligent Avalanche, and the windfall's guilt-free slice, Windfalls — plans with zero joy get abandoned, and an abandoned plan protects nothing).

Cut mercilessly everything you don't. The target isn't the beloved spending — it's the middle tier of meh: purchases that are merely fine, kept by habit or mild social pressure, delivering little per dollar. The default restaurant no one craves. The subscription tier optimism selected. The upgrade that upgraded nothing felt. This tier is where Spending as a Mirror's habitual pile lives, and it's cuttable without loss — which is what makes it, not the lattes, the real budget frontier.

Most budgets do the exact reverse: trim everything evenly, which starves the joy that makes discipline sustainable while preserving the mediocre bulk. Even-trimming feels fair and fails reliably — by March, the budget that cut the beloved dinner 15% along with everything else is abandoned, and the meh spending survives the wreckage untouched.

The arithmetic of concentration

Two people, identical $800 a month of discretionary spend. One: $500 concentrated on a genuine passion, $300 on essentials-adjacent basics — a life with a visible, funded center. The other: $800 sprayed evenly across forty meh transactions — nothing loved, nothing cut, nothing felt. Same outflow, wildly different lives — and the first person can find $200 of cuts without touching anything they'd miss, because their spending has a structure with a "don't care" tier in it. Concentration doesn't just buy more joy per dollar; it makes every future adjustment cheaper, because the map of what matters already exists.

Where Plenee fits

Plenee doesn't grade your categories — no morality tags on dining or travel, no applause for austerity. It shows you the allocation: what you actually funded, in what proportions, against your own history. Whether the allocation matches your values is a judgment only you can make — and the only one that matters. The tool's job is making the allocation visible enough that the judgment is possible; the smuggled-values business is left to other people's budgets.

The takeaway

There is no universal good spend — there's only alignment between your money and your honest joys. Fund what you love without apology; cut the meh without mercy; let the savings rate be a pleasure if it genuinely is one; and ignore anyone's formula — including this one — that argues with your honest joy. The budget that survives is the one with your actual life in it.
